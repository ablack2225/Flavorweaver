# Flavorweaver House Rules

Core Amy & Don kitchen rules that should guide recipe development, testing, and canon decisions.

## Seafood Flavor Rule: Clean Seafood, Never Fishy-Funky

Amy and Don like **clean seafood sweetness** and savory depth, but they do **not** like fishy, funky, anchovy-heavy, stale-ocean, or aggressively fermented seafood flavors.

### Why this rule exists

This rule was confirmed during the Whole-Tamarind Shrimp Pad Thai live test. The sauce became unpleasant to Amy and Don when several seafood-forward elements stacked together:

- A disliked fish sauce had to be used after the preferred fish sauce ran out.
- Fish sauce was present in the main sauce and may have also touched the shrimp seasoning or side sauce.
- Shrimp-shell concentrate was already adding seafood depth.
- Dried shrimp was planned as an additional umami layer.
- The sauce did not yet have enough sweet tamarind/date/honey body to cushion the fishy edge.

The problem was not seafood itself. The problem was **fishy/funky dominance**. Amy and Don prefer bold, spicy, tangy, saucy, deeply seasoned food, but the savory base must stay clean and appetizing.

### Safe seafood lane

Use these confidently, with balance:

- Fresh shrimp, crab, fish, or other seafood with a clean sweet flavor.
- Light shrimp-shell or seafood concentrates in small amounts.
- High-quality clean fish sauce in restrained quantities.
- Soy sauce, dark soy sauce, oyster sauce, mushroom sauce, or broth as safer savory bridges.
- Tamarind, lime, rice vinegar, honey, brown sugar, dates, aromatics, and chiles to round and brighten seafood dishes.

### Use caution or avoid

Limit heavily unless Amy explicitly approves a test:

- Strong or low-quality fish sauce.
- Dried shrimp as a major flavor driver.
- Shrimp paste.
- Anchovy-forward sauces.
- Fermented seafood pastes.
- Fish sauce-heavy traditional profiles.
- Any component described as funky, pungent, intensely fermented, or fishy.

### Default design correction

When adapting seafood dishes for Flavorweaver, start from this assumption:

> Build clean seafood sweetness and savory depth, but avoid fishy funk. Fish sauce should usually be a background seasoning, not the dominant flavor.

For Pad Thai-style sauces, the Amy & Don house direction should lean toward:

> Tamarind-date sweet-sour depth, Thai chile heat, soy-based savory balance, peanuts, lime, and only a whisper of clean fish sauce.

### Practical recipe defaults

- Start with **1 to 2 Tbsp good fish sauce max** for a two-person noodle dish unless there is a clear reason to go higher.
- Use **soy sauce as part of the savory base** when fishiness is a risk.
- Keep shrimp-shell concentrate to **1 Tbsp max** until tasted.
- Treat dried shrimp as optional and start at **1 tsp or less**, not tablespoons.
- If fishiness appears, stop adding seafood ingredients and correct with tamarind/date/honey/sugar, soy sauce, water, lime, and chile depending the dish.

## Flavorweaver Canon Reminder

Flavorweaver recipes should be built for Amy and Don's actual palate, not for generic tradition. Traditional technique and ingredients are useful only when they create a 9.5/10+ Amy-and-Don result.
