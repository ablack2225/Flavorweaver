Teff Starter & Real Injera

A traditional-style fermented teff flatbread project built from an active teff starter and a fermented injera batter.

Prep30m active
Cook30-45m

Kitchen Execution Summary

Project Style

This is a multi-day fermentation project, not a quick bread. Build an active teff starter first, then ferment the injera batter.

Measurement Style

Use grams when possible for the starter. Approximate cup and spoon measurements are included for cooks without a scale.

Cooking Style

Cook thin rounds on a nonstick skillet, crepe pan, or flat griddle with a lid. Injera cooks covered and is not flipped.

Texture Target

Soft, flexible, tangy, spongy, and holey enough to soak up stews.

Required Components

Teff Starter

Teff flour and filtered or non-chlorinated water, fed daily until bubbly and pleasantly tangy.

Injera Batter

Teff flour, water, active teff starter, and salt added after fermentation.

Cooking Surface

Use a large nonstick skillet, crepe pan, or flat griddle with a lid.

Time

Allow 4 to 7 days for starter activity and 24 to 72 hours for batter fermentation.

Full Project Ingredient Roll-Up

Teff Starter

For the initial culture and daily feeding: 50g teff flour, about 1/3 cup + 1 Tbsp, and 50g filtered or non-chlorinated water, about 3 Tbsp + 1 tsp.

Injera Batter

2 cups teff flour, 2 1/2 to 3 cups water plus more as needed for thinning, 1/4 cup active teff starter, and 1/2 tsp salt added after fermentation.

Fermentation & Prep Map

Starter feed: use 50g teff flour and 50g water, or approximately 1/3 cup + 1 Tbsp teff flour and 3 Tbsp + 1 tsp water.

Starter timing: feed once daily for 4 to 7 days, longer if the kitchen is cool.

Batter timing: ferment the mixed batter for 24 to 72 hours, depending on warmth and desired tang.

Cooking station: prepare a nonstick skillet or crepe pan, a lid, a towel or plate for cooked injera, and a small amount of water for thinning if needed.

Stage-Based Prep Bowls

Jar 1 — Teff Starter

50g / about 1/3 cup + 1 Tbsp teff flour and 50g / about 3 Tbsp + 1 tsp filtered water. Mix into a thick batter or loose paste.

Daily Feeding

Keep part of the starter and refresh with 50g teff flour and 50g water, using the same approximate volume measurements if needed.

Bowl 1 — Injera Batter

2 cups teff flour, 2 1/2 cups water, and 1/4 cup active teff starter. Hold extra water for thinning later.

Bowl 2 — Pre-Cook Finish

1/2 tsp salt and enough extra water to thin the fermented batter until it pours like heavy cream or thin crepe batter.

Do Not Forget

Do not rush the starter. Use it when it bubbles reliably, smells pleasantly tangy, and shows activity after feeding.

Add salt after batter fermentation, not before.

Cook injera covered and do not flip it.

Treat the first injera as the heat and batter-thickness test piece.

If the batter cracks, it may be too thick or the pan may be too hot. If it lacks bubbles, it may need more fermentation, more active starter, or better heat control.

Full Fermentation Timeline and Instructions

  1. Start the teff starter. Mix 50g teff flour, about 1/3 cup + 1 Tbsp, with 50g filtered or non-chlorinated water, about 3 Tbsp + 1 tsp, in a clean glass jar. Stir into a thick batter or loose paste, scrape down the sides, and cover loosely.
  2. Feed daily. Feed once per day for 4 to 7 days: keep part of the starter and refresh with 50g teff flour and 50g water, using the same approximate volume measurements if needed.
  3. Watch for readiness. Use the starter when it bubbles reliably, smells pleasantly tangy, and shows activity after feeding.
  4. Mix the injera batter. Whisk 2 cups teff flour, 2 1/2 cups water, and 1/4 cup active teff starter in a large bowl. The batter should be thinner than pancake batter but not watery.
  5. Ferment the batter. Cover loosely and ferment at room temperature for 24 to 72 hours. Ferment longer for more tang or cook sooner for a milder result once the batter is bubbly.
  6. Finish before cooking. Stir the fermented batter well, add 1/2 tsp salt, and thin with water as needed until it pours like heavy cream or thin crepe batter.
  7. Heat the pan. Heat a large nonstick skillet, crepe pan, or flat griddle over medium heat. Use no oil or only the thinnest wiped film if needed.
  8. Cook without flipping. Pour in enough batter to make a thin round, swirl quickly, cover immediately, and cook 1 1/2 to 3 minutes until the top is dry, bubbles have formed, and the surface looks spongy. Do not flip.
  9. Repeat. Gently remove and place on a towel or plate. Repeat with remaining batter, treating the first injera as the heat, timing, and batter-thickness test piece.

Final Taste Target

Injera should be soft, flexible, tangy, and spongy with small holes that soak up stews. It should not be crisp, browned like naan, or flipped during cooking.

Chef's Notes

For the practical Doro Wat platter, naan can be used as a scoop-bread substitute. For the traditional-style Ethiopian version, use this teff starter and real injera process, allowing enough time for the starter and batter fermentation.

This should remain a dedicated Ethiopian bread project, not a quick bread substitute.
