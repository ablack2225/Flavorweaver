# Pork Loin Schnitzel with Rotkohl

> Thin pork-loin schnitzel fried golden and crisp, served with glossy sweet-sour German red cabbage made from purple cabbage, apple, onion, vinegar, brown sugar, and warm spice.

## Flavor Target

The schnitzel should be thin, crisp, golden, savory, and tender inside. The breading should puff and crunch lightly, not become greasy or heavy.

The Rotkohl should be soft and glossy, sweet-sour, gently spiced, buttery but not greasy, and tender without becoming mushy. It should taste like cabbage, apple, vinegar, brown sugar, and warm spice in balance.

## Before Cooking Day / Advance Prep

- Slice the pork loin into cutlets ahead if desired.
- Rotkohl can be made a day ahead; it often tastes even better after resting.
- If making potatoes, start them before breading the schnitzel.
- Set up a rack over a sheet pan for resting breaded schnitzel and draining fried schnitzel.

## Cross-Component Prep Map

- **Pork loin:** slice across the loin into cutlets before pounding. Thin, even cutlets are the key to tender schnitzel.
- **Breadcrumbs:** panko gives crunch, plain breadcrumbs give classic coverage. A half-and-half mix is ideal.
- **Cabbage:** thin slicing creates silky Rotkohl; thick slicing makes the braise take longer.
- **Apple:** Granny Smith gives tart balance against brown sugar and vinegar.
- **Acid and sugar:** Rotkohl must be adjusted at the end. Too sharp gets sugar; too sweet gets vinegar; flat gets salt.
- **Timing:** Rotkohl can wait gently; schnitzel should be fried close to service.

## Do Not Forget

- Use boneless pork loin for economy, but slice and pound it thin so it eats like schnitzel, not a pork chop.
- Pound pork to about **1/4 inch thick**.
- Let breaded schnitzel rest **10 minutes** before frying.
- Fry quickly around **350°F** so the crust browns before the pork dries out.
- Rotkohl needs enough time to soften fully. Rushed cabbage tastes raw and sharp.
- Add mustard to the cabbage only at the end and only if it needs depth.
- Serve schnitzel immediately after frying; Rotkohl can wait warm.

## Full Cooking Timeline and Instructions

| Step | Action |
|---:|---|
| 1 | **Start the Rotkohl.** Core and thin slice **1 medium head purple/red cabbage**. Thin slice **1 medium yellow onion**. Peel and grate or finely dice **1 Granny Smith apple**. |
| 2 | **Sauté onion.** In a Dutch oven or deep skillet, melt **3 Tbsp butter, bacon fat, or a mix** over medium heat. Add the onion and cook **4 to 5 minutes**, until softened. |
| 3 | **Add cabbage and apple.** Add the sliced cabbage, apple, **1 tsp kosher salt**, **1/4 tsp black pepper**, **1 bay leaf**, optional tiny pinch cloves, and optional tiny pinch cinnamon. Stir well. |
| 4 | **Add sweet-sour liquid.** Add **1/3 cup apple cider vinegar**, **2 Tbsp brown sugar**, and **1/2 cup apple juice, apple cider, chicken broth, or water**. Stir. Reserve the extra **1 Tbsp brown sugar** for adjustment. |
| 5 | **Braise Rotkohl.** Cover and cook on low to medium-low for **45 to 75 minutes**, stirring occasionally, until tender and glossy. Uncover for the last **10 to 15 minutes** if too liquidy. |
| 6 | **Slice pork loin.** Slice **1 1/4 to 1 1/2 lb boneless pork loin** into **4 cutlets**, about 5 to 6 oz each. Pound each cutlet to about **1/4 inch thick**. |
| 7 | **Season pork.** Mix **1 tsp kosher salt**, **1/2 tsp black pepper**, **1/2 tsp garlic powder**, **1/2 tsp plain paprika**, and **1/4 tsp onion powder**. Season both sides of the cutlets. |
| 8 | **Set breading station.** Dish 1: **1/2 cup flour**. Dish 2: **2 eggs** beaten with **1 Tbsp milk or water**. Dish 3: **1 1/2 cups breadcrumbs or panko** with a pinch salt and pinch pepper. |
| 9 | **Bread schnitzel.** Dredge pork in flour, dip in egg wash, then coat in breadcrumbs. Press lightly, but do not pack the coating too hard. Rest on a rack for **10 minutes**. |
| 10 | **Balance Rotkohl.** Taste cabbage. If too sharp, add the remaining **1 Tbsp brown sugar**. If too sweet, add a splash of vinegar. If flat, add salt. If it needs depth, add **1 to 2 tsp Dijon or stone-ground mustard**. Remove bay leaf. |
| 11 | **Fry schnitzel.** Heat neutral oil to about **350°F**. Fry schnitzel **2 to 3 minutes per side**, until golden and cooked through. Add **1 to 2 Tbsp butter** near the end if desired for flavor. Drain on a rack and lightly salt while hot. |
| 12 | **Plate.** Serve schnitzel immediately with warm Rotkohl, lemon wedges, and mashed potatoes, roasted potatoes, buttered noodles, or spaetzle-style noodles. |

## Rotkohl Balance Map

| Issue | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| Too sharp | Add brown sugar, 1 tsp at a time. |
| Too sweet | Add apple cider vinegar, 1 tsp at a time. |
| Flat | Add salt. |
| Too watery | Simmer uncovered longer. |
| Too heavy | Add a tiny splash vinegar. |
| Missing depth | Add 1 tsp Dijon or stone-ground mustard. |

## Pork Loin Notes

A whole boneless pork loin is the cost-effective choice. For schnitzel, slice only what you need into cutlets and freeze the rest in meal-sized portions. Cutlets should be pounded thin so they fry quickly and stay tender.

## Plating Plan

Place schnitzel slightly off-center with lemon wedges nearby. Spoon Rotkohl beside it so the glossy purple cabbage shows. Add potatoes or noodles as the starch. Keep any gravy or mustard sauce optional for a later test; the first version should let the schnitzel and Rotkohl define the plate.

## Live-Test Notes to Capture

- Did boneless pork loin stay tender after pounding and frying?
- Was the breadcrumb texture better with panko, plain breadcrumbs, or a half-and-half mix?
- Did Rotkohl need 2 Tbsp or 3 Tbsp brown sugar?
- Did mustard improve the cabbage or distract from the sweet-sour target?
- Which starch did Don prefer with the plate?
