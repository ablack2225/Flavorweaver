Teff Starter & Real Injera

A traditional-style fermented teff flatbread project built from an active teff starter and a fermented injera batter.

Prep30m active
Cook30-45m
TotalMulti-day project
Yield8-12 injera
TeffFermentedTraditional technique

Ingredient List

Teff Starter

Teff flour and filtered or non-chlorinated water.

Injera Batter

Teff flour, water, active teff starter, and salt.

Cooking

Large nonstick skillet, crepe pan, or flat griddle with a lid.

Measured Ingredients

Teff Starter

50gTeff flourInitial culture and daily feeding.
50gFiltered or non-chlorinated waterInitial culture and daily feeding.

Injera Batter

2 cupsTeff flour
2 1/2-3 cupsWaterPlus more as needed for thinning.
1/4 cupActive teff starterBubbly and pleasantly tangy.
1/2 tspSaltAdd after fermentation.

Instructions

  1. Mix 50g teff flour and 50g water in a clean glass jar. Cover loosely and leave at room temperature.
  2. Feed once per day for 4-7 days by keeping part of the starter and refreshing with teff flour and water.
  3. Use the starter when it bubbles reliably, smells pleasantly tangy, and shows activity after feeding.
  4. Whisk teff flour, water, and active starter into a loose batter. Cover loosely and ferment 24-72 hours.
  5. Before cooking, stir well, add salt, and thin with water until it pours like heavy cream or thin crepe batter.
  6. Heat a nonstick skillet or crepe pan over medium heat. Use no oil or only the thinnest wiped film if needed.
  7. Pour a thin round, swirl quickly, cover immediately, and cook until the top is dry, bubbly, and spongy. Do not flip.
  8. Remove gently and repeat. Treat the first injera as the heat and batter-thickness test piece.

Chef's Notes

For the practical Doro Wat platter, naan can be used as a scoop-bread substitute. For the traditional-style Ethiopian version, use this teff starter and real injera process, allowing enough time for the starter and batter fermentation.

Injera should be soft, flexible, tangy, and spongy with small holes that soak up stews. It should not be crisp, browned like naan, or flipped during cooking.
