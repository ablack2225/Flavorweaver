Bánh Mì Không-Style Dipping Rolls

Vietnamese-style short dipping rolls with a thin crackly crust, light airy interior, and enough structure to tear and dip into Bò Kho broth.

Prep20 min
Rise1 hr 35 min-2 hr 15 min
Bake18-22 min
Yield4 small rolls
bánh mìactive dry yeastcrisp crustairy crumbbò kho pairing

Kitchen Execution Summary

Meal Role

Fresh dipping bread for Vietnamese Bò Kho-style brisket stew.

Best Texture

Thin crisp crust, airy interior, light chew.

Key Technique

Active dry yeast bloom at 105°F-108°F, gentle counter shaping, covered final proof, hot oven, steam.

Timing Rule

Start dough early; bake close to serving.

Measured Ingredients

Dough

2 cupsAll-purpose flourKeeps crumb lighter.
1 cupBread flourAdds dipping structure and chew.
2 tspActive dry yeastBloom before mixing.
1 tspSugarFeeds yeast.
1 1/4 cupsWaterUse 1 cup for bloom, remaining as needed.
1 1/2 tspFine salt
1 TbspNeutral oil
1 tspFresh lime juiceSupports dough structure and brightness.
1 cupHot waterFor oven steam.

Yeast Bloom Note

For the best active dry yeast bloom, use water between 105°F and 108°F. Check with a thermometer when possible. Water that is too cool may slow or weaken the rise; water that is too hot can damage the yeast.

Prep Notes

Shape lightly on the counter. Do not roll the dough like a meatball in your hands.

Cover shaped rolls during the final rise so the surface does not dry out.

Bake close to serving so the crust stays crisp.

Stage-Based Prep Bowls

Yeast Bowl

1 cup water at 105°F-108°F, sugar, active dry yeast.

Dry Bowl

All-purpose flour, bread flour, salt.

Dough Additions

Neutral oil, lime juice, remaining water as needed.

Bake Setup

Parchment, sharp knife, cover for shaped rise, metal steam pan, hot water.

Instructions

  1. Bloom the yeast by combining 1 cup water between 105°F and 108°F, sugar, and active dry yeast. Let sit 5-10 minutes, until foamy. If the yeast does not foam, stop and restart with fresh yeast.
  2. Add all-purpose flour, bread flour, salt, neutral oil, and lime juice. Mix until a shaggy dough forms. Add up to 1/4 cup additional warm water, 1 Tbsp at a time, only if dry.
  3. Knead by hand for 5-7 minutes, until smoother and elastic. Keep the dough soft and slightly tacky, not stiff.
  4. Place dough in a lightly oiled bowl. Cover and let rise 60-90 minutes, until doubled.
  5. Turn dough onto a lightly floured surface and divide into 4 pieces. Shape each piece on the counter, not like rolling a meatball in your hands: gently flatten it into a small oval or rectangle, fold the top third down toward the center and press the seam lightly, fold the bottom third up over it, pinch the seam closed, then place it seam-side down. With both hands, gently roll from the center outward on the counter until it forms a short baguette or torpedo shape, about 5-6 inches long. If the dough springs back, rest it 5-10 minutes, then finish shaping.
  6. Place rolls on a parchment-lined baking sheet. Cover loosely with lightly oiled plastic wrap, or use a clean kitchen towel if the kitchen is not dry. Use a lightly damp towel if the air is dry. Let rise 35-45 minutes, until puffy and slightly expanded. The rolls do not need to fully double after shaping.
  7. Preheat oven to 450°F with an empty metal pan on the lower rack for steam.
  8. Uncover the rolls. Slash each roll lengthwise with a sharp knife just before baking. Place rolls in the oven, pour 1 cup hot water into the steam pan, and close the oven immediately. Vent steam carefully when opening later.
  9. Bake 18-22 minutes, until deeply golden, crisp, and light-feeling when lifted.
  10. Cool 10-15 minutes before tearing open. Serve warm with Bò Kho for dipping.

Troubleshooting / Rescue Notes

If yeast does not foam: restart with fresh yeast and water between 105°F and 108°F.

If dough feels stiff: add warm water 1 Tbsp at a time until soft and slightly tacky.

If dough springs back during shaping: rest 5-10 minutes, then continue shaping.

If the surface dries during final rise: cover with lightly oiled plastic wrap or a lightly damp towel.

If rolls are dense: shape more gently next time and avoid over-flouring.

If crust is dull or soft: use steam at the start and bake until deeply golden.

Chef's Notes

These rolls are designed for Bò Kho broth. They should not be enriched like soft dinner rolls and should not be seasoned with herbs. The flavor should stay clean so the bread can carry the stew.

All-purpose flour keeps the interior light. Bread flour adds enough structure for dipping.
