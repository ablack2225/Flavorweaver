Ethiopian-Inspired Doro Wat Platter

A practical complete meal plate with berbere-style whole chicken thighs and eggs, yellow split pea alicha, atakilt wat, timatim salad, and naan as a scoop-bread substitute, with Misir Wat and real injera preserved as the traditional upgrade path.

Plating & Serving Architecture

Per plate: serve 1 whole chicken thigh, 2 sauced eggs, a generous spoon of split pea alicha for the working plate or Misir Wat for the traditional red-lentil direction, a spoon of atakilt wat, a bright spoon of timatim salad, and warm naan or injera for scooping.

Visual build: place the scoop bread as the foundation or to the side, then set the whole chicken thigh and eggs slightly off-center with sauce spooned over the top. Add the golden alicha or red Misir Wat beside it, tuck the soft vegetable atakilt wat near the stew, and keep the timatim salad fresh and separate so it stays bright.

Eating experience: each bite should move between spicy rich sauce, tender whole-thigh chicken, creamy stew, soft warm vegetables, and cool lemony salad. The bread should make the plate feel interactive, scoopable, and restaurant-style.

Instructions

  1. Day before: measure and mix the House Berbere-Style Spice Blend. Use 1 Tbsp for the marination and reserve 1 Tbsp additional blend for the sauce base. Store any leftover blend airtight.
  2. Day before: mix the House Berbere-Style Marination ingredients into a paste, coat the whole chicken thighs thoroughly, cover, and refrigerate overnight. Leave the thighs whole; do not chop or dice them.
  3. Bread prep: prepare naan dough early if using homemade naan. If using real injera, plan it as a multi-day fermentation project and have it ready before cooking day assembly.
  4. Start first on cooking day: begin Split Pea Alicha because it needs the longest steady simmer. If making Misir Wat instead, it cooks faster and can be started while the chicken simmers.
  5. Hard-boil, peel, and lightly score the eggs.
  6. Cook the diced onion until softened, reduced, and beginning to turn golden.
  7. Add butter or oil, then bloom the additional House Berbere-Style Spice Blend briefly. Do not burn the spices.
  8. Add garlic, ginger, and tomato paste. Cook until fragrant and deepened.
  9. Add the whole marinated chicken thighs and sear lightly on both sides. Keep the thighs whole while searing.
  10. Add broth and simmer the thighs whole, covered on low, until the chicken is tender and cooked through.
  11. While the chicken simmers, cook Atakilt Wat. If using Misir Wat, cook it during this window as well.
  12. Add eggs during the last part of cooking and spoon sauce over them.
  13. Simmer uncovered until the sauce thickens and clings to the whole thighs and eggs. Adjust seasoning as needed.
  14. Make Timatim Salad near the end so it stays cool, bright, and fresh.
  15. Cook or rewarm prepared naan close to serving, or keep injera covered so it stays flexible.
  16. Assemble each plate with naan or injera, 1 whole doro wat-style chicken thigh, 2 eggs per person, split pea alicha or Misir Wat, atakilt wat, timatim salad, and extra sauce spooned over the whole thigh and eggs.

Chef's Notes

This recipe is intentionally Ethiopian-inspired. The first working version uses practical substitutions for ingredients that can be expensive, delayed, or hard to source in the U.S.

Yellow split pea alicha is the repeatable working side because it is accessible, creamy, mild, and a good counterpoint to the spicy chicken. For the classic red-lentil direction, use Misir Wat.

Naan is the practical scoop-bread substitute for this first version. For the traditional-style Ethiopian version, use Teff Starter & Real Injera, allowing enough time for the starter and batter fermentation.

Because the chicken thighs are small, this version includes 4 eggs total, or 2 eggs per person, so the plate still feels generous. The thighs are cooked and plated whole; do not chop or dice them for this platter.
